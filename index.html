STREAMOCRACY

How streaming changed filmaking

In less than twenty years streaming services like Netflix, Prime Video, Hulu and many others have changed the way we watch movies and tv shows, they affected our routines and our culture so thoroughly that they entered our language.

Binge-watching runner-up for Oxford Dictionary’s “word of the year 2013”

By 2015, Netflix and chill had become an Internet meme.

Streaming services challenged the traditional entertainment industry, by creating new business models for the production, distribution, and release of TV series and movies. The new model includes:

  • monthly subscription plan to access the contents
  • broadcasting content directly on the web
  • algorithm that tracks user choices and suggests related content

Their dominant position, places Internet platforms in a strong decisional position in terms of film content. In the future, this kind of authority could be key in determining what constitutes a "well-made film"...

But has streaming really changed the movie industry?

In oreder to answer our question start from two existing datasets:

Netflix: One of the most popular media and video streaming platforms with over 200M Subscribers. Our Netflix dataset contains data on the over 8000 movies and tv shows available on their platform up to 2021, along with data on: cast, directors, ratings, release year, duration, etc

Amazon prime: popular media and video streaming platforms created by Amazon with over 200M Subscribers globally. Our Prime Video dataset consists of data on all the movies and tv shows available on Amazon Prime Video up to 2021.

We end up with a dataset made by:

8313 titles
6990 directors
6832 distributors
7449 countries
7460 languages

Starting from our data and our research question: how streaming platforms have changed film production; we decided to divide the analysis into 3 steps:

  1. What kind of content is present on Streaming Platforms?
  2. How streaming services treats their content?
  3. How streaming service influence cinema industry?

What kind of content is present on Streaming Platforms?

More movies than you’d expect

Our dataset contains over 8.000 entries from Netflix and from Amazon, which covers movies added from the creation of these platforms to 2021 Despite being better known for their tv Series both Amazon and Netflix contain more than twice as many movies.

Movies from all over the world

Content comes from all over the world! Despite that, more than 40% of movies are from the USA and more than 50% of movies are shot in English

Who are the directors?

We used Gender-guesser phyton’s library to determine the gender of the directors by their names, this library can fail sometimes as it is mostly trained on Anglo-Saxon names, thus we must take into account a margin of error. Nonetheless, more than 80% of all directors resulted to be men. Of the rest approximately 4% remain unknown, 12% are women and only 2 authors are recorded as transgender females and 2 as nonbinary.

Who are the directors?

The number of female directors grows over the years and reaches its peaks in 2017 and 2020, but it probably follows the general trend of movie additions that grows over the years and decreases in 2020, likely for the impact that the Covid-19 pandemic had on the entertainment industry. This means that while women increase so do men and the proportion between men and women directors remains mostly unchanged over the years.

What about the target?

Movie and tv series follow different rating systems that depend on the country, the distribution, and the producers, here we used a grouping system displayed on Amazon Prime Video(link?) We can see that the bigger portion of the content is aimed at adults in both series and movies, and the quantity of content decreases with the age of the target, an exception is the age group between 13 and 16, very targeted by movies and almost completely discarded by tv series.

How streaming services treats their content?

Netflix over the years

Netflix’s rise in notoriety and success started in 2013 as we can see from the graph the following years from 2015 to 2020 have a steep increase in series and especially in movie additions. in 2020 the trend inverts and there is a decrease in additions, this is likely caused by the impact the covid-19 pandemic had on cinematographic productions, but also by the concurrence made to Netflix by many other companies imitating Netflix and reproducing its model. In some cases this meant for Netflix some important renounces, for example When Disney+ made its debut, it meant the end of Disney blockbusters, such as the Star Wars films, being available on Netflix or other platforms.

Amazon over the years

Amazon, on the other hand, has had a more steady trend of movies and series additions since its creation in 2006, it is interesting to notice that in the latest year movie additions dropped but series additions are increasing, to showcase probably a new decision on what content to release.

How streaming service influence cinema industry?

Who are the distributors

Our dataset represents data from films hosted on Prime Video and Netflix platforms, the data on distributors refers to the company that initially distributed the movie, so we expect to find all the movies distributed by Netflix and prime video along with data on the movie that were distributed by other companies and later added to Netflix and Amazon. Therefore it is expected to see Prime Video and Netflix as major distributors in this dataset. However, Netflix appears to be the most prominent distributor, and this shows that it really became an important presence in the entertainment industry.

Genres and Distributors

Every distributors has its personality, and privileges different genres, Amzon is particolarly fond of Drama, while Netflix's strong suit are International shows.

Who wins the prizes?

Netflix and Amazon aren’t strong distributors of movies prized by important awards, But they are both receiving many prizes in the category of awards that represent smaller and fewer know prizes on our dataset. Until 2020 the Academy awards rules stipulated that a movie needed to have a theatrical release to be able to win an oscar and many Movie released on Streaming platforms are considered “Tv movies”, so the question is where or not they are eligible for certain prizes is still discussed by the cinema community

...And what prizes do they win?

Covid-19 pandemic theatres were forced to close and the Academy to revise their rules, at the same time streaming platforms are releasing more and more movies loved by the critique and that receive important prizes as can be seen by this heatmap.

Critics vs the Public

To understand what the critique and the public think of the movies we use two measures: Rotten Tomatoes scores: Rottentomatoes is a website that aggregates critics’ reviews of movies and it is widely considered a reliable measure of critics’ appreciation of a movie IMDb Scores: the Internet Movie Database is the biggest database of metadata on movies and series on the web, it allows anyone to post reviews on the titles contained on the database, so it can be considered a good measure of public opinion. If we watch how these indexes evolve over the years we can notice that until the 90’s both indexes followed a similar pattern of peaks and depressions, with the critique being overall more enthusiastic about movies than the public. Between the ’90s and 2010’s the public and the critics diverged, with the public being more generous with movies. Fnally, in the last decade, the trends changed again but with both critics and public opinion growing more appreciative of movies. There are several factors that can explain this phenomenon, (for instance both IMDb and Rotten Tomatoes are very recent, so the opinion of old movies is mostly retroactive).

the Public vs the Critics

Anyway, it is clear that critics’ opinions and public opinions of movies are undergoing a transformation over the last 20 years and it is likely that this also depends on the new ways of movie consumption, introduced by streaming services over the last years.
